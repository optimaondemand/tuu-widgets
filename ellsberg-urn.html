The Mix Revealed

The Ellsberg Paradox

Most people prefer Gamble A over B (known over unknown probability) AND Gamble D over C (known over unknown probability). This feels intuitively safe — prefer what you know.

But here's the problem: If you assign any probability p to drawing black, then:
• A>B means P(red) > P(black) → 30/90 > p/90 → p < 30
• D>C means P(B+W) > P(R+W) → 60/90 > (30+white)/90 → white < 30 → black > 30 → p > 30

These two inequalities cannot both be true! No single probability assignment satisfies A>B and D>C simultaneously. This is ambiguity aversion — a preference for known over unknown risks that cannot be explained by expected utility theory.
