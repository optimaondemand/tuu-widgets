The Setup

In 1670, Blaise Pascal posed a decision-theory argument: if God exists and belief leads to infinite reward while disbelief leads to infinite punishment, then — even if the probability of God's existence is very small — expected utility theory says you should believe.

The Many Gods Objection

What if there are multiple possible gods with conflicting reward structures? For example: God A rewards belief, but God B punishes belief (because God B values intellectual honesty and only rewards non-believers).

If both have nonzero probability, then EU(Believe) includes +∞ (from God A) and −∞ (from God B). In mathematics, ∞ − ∞ is undefined — not a number you can compare.

EU(Believe) = +∞ + (−∞) = UNDEFINED The wager loses its mathematical force
God A: P(A)·∞ = +∞ (belief rewarded)
God B: P(B)·(−∞) = −∞ (belief punished)
EU(Believe) = +∞ + (−∞) = UNDEFINED
EU(Don't Believe) = −∞ + +∞ = UNDEFINED

→ No rational choice can be derived from undefined EU values.

This is one of the classical objections to Pascal's Wager. Once you allow for multiple incompatible infinite-payoff scenarios, the entire EU framework breaks down. The argument requires you to treat one specific theological scenario as the only one worth considering.

What if the payoffs were finite?

What this tells us about EU theory

Pascal's Wager is one of the earliest known applications of expected utility reasoning — predating Bernoulli and von Neumann by decades. It reveals a genuine weakness in EU theory: when payoffs can be infinite, the framework produces paradoxes.

Any nonzero probability multiplied by ∞ equals ∞, so a single infinite outcome swamps all other considerations, regardless of how tiny its probability is. This is a feature, not a bug — it's the same logic that makes the St. Petersburg paradox work.

Modern decision theory handles this in several ways: by bounding utility functions, using lexicographic preferences, or requiring that infinite utilities never actually occur. The wager is a powerful case study for understanding when and why the EU framework needs limits.
