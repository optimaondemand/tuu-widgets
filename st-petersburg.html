The St. Petersburg Game

Expected value = ∞ — but would you really pay a fortune to play?

How the Game Works

Flip a fair coin repeatedly until you get Tails. Count how many flips you made. If the first flip is Tails (n=1), you win $2. Each extra Heads doubles your prize.

Most games end quickly — but occasionally you get a spectacular run of Heads!

The St. Petersburg Paradox

The expected monetary value of this game is literally infinite — yet almost nobody would pay more than $20–30 to play. Why? Because utility is not linear with money. The 1,000th dollar feels less valuable than the 1st.

Daniel Bernoulli (1738) proposed that people maximize expected utility, not expected money — and that utility grows logarithmically. With U(x) = log(x), the game's expected utility is finite, matching our intuitions about a fair entry fee.

This paradox was one of the first arguments for utility theory — the foundation of everything we study in this course.
